* The Story

It started with a YouTube video.

A friend and fellow creator was building a walkie talkie using Glyph
boards. It worked — technically. But it was held together on a
breadboard, and anyone who has worked with breadboards knows the
struggle: loose connections, noise, wires everywhere, and the constant
question of "why did it just stop working?"

That video planted a seed.

I had been wanting to learn PCB design for a while, and this felt like
the perfect excuse. Instead of designing a full walkie talkie board
from scratch — which would be insane for a first project — I could
design a *carrier board*. A single board that brings the Glyph, the
audio codec, the amplifier, the microphone, the battery management,
and all the supporting circuitry together into one clean package.

It seemed simple enough. A perfect beginner project. A great piece of
content. Maybe a week of work.

I was wrong. And I'm glad I was.

What followed was one of the most intense, frustrating, and rewarding
learning experiences I have ever had. I learned about schematics and
how to read datasheets. I learned about component placement and why
"just connect the pins" is not a routing strategy. I learned about
power distribution, ground planes, decoupling capacitors, and why your
microcontroller resets when the transmitter keys up if you get it
wrong. I learned about buses, differential pairs, ERC and DRC errors,
footprint libraries, design rules, manufacturing constraints, and a
hundred other things I didn't even know existed two weeks earlier.

There were moments when I felt completely stuck. Moments where I
thought I had finished, only to realize I needed to tear something up
and start over. I learned very quickly that a good schematic does not
automatically become a good PCB. The schematic is the vision; the
layout is where reality pushes back.

Thankfully, I was not alone. I had mentors who patiently guided me
through the process. Every time I thought I had figured something out,
they would point out a better way, explain the reasoning behind it,
and push me to think like an engineer, not just a hobbyist following a
tutorial. They reviewed my designs, answered my questions at odd
hours, and made sure I was heading in the right direction.

Today, the board has officially been sent for manufacturing. Seeing
the final design and clicking that "Order" button gave me goosebumps.
Whether it works perfectly on the first try or needs revisions, I have
already learned more than I ever expected.

This is not just a PCB. It is proof that you can start from zero and
build something real.

* What is teleVox?

teleVox is a DIY walkie talkie built around the [[https://www.instagram.com/reezen.xyz/][Glyph]] ecosystem. It is
designed as a carrier board — a custom PCB that integrates off-the-shelf
modules into a single, compact, battery-powered handheld device.

The goal is simple: a walkie talkie that actually works reliably, with
a proper PCB instead of a breadboard, enclosed in a 3D printed shell,
powered by a rechargeable battery.

* How It Works

The system is built around these core blocks:

- *Glyph C3* — the brain. An ESP32-C3 based development board that
  handles the digital audio processing and wireless communication.
- *Audio Codec (ES8388)* — converts analog audio from the microphone
  to digital (ADC) and digital audio back to analog (DAC) for the
  speaker.
- *Microphone* — a MEMS microphone with on-board amplifier for clear
  voice capture.
- *Audio Amplifier (MAX98357)* — a Class-D amplifier that drives the
  speaker with enough volume to be heard clearly.
- *Battery Management* — a TP4056 based charging circuit with
  protection, allowing the device to run on a single 18650 Li-Ion cell
  and charge via USB-C.
- *Power Regulation* — buck and boost converters to provide stable
  3.3V and 5V rails from the battery.
- *User Interface* — physical buttons for PTT (Push-To-Talk), volume,
  and channel selection, plus status LEDs.
- *3D Printed Enclosure* — a custom shell that houses everything in a
  handheld form factor.

* The Design

The PCB was designed in [[https://www.kicad.org/][KiCad]], an open-source EDA tool. The design
went through several iterations:

1. *Schematic Capture* — drawing up the circuit, selecting components,
   checking pinouts against datasheets, and running electrical rules
   checks (ERC).
2. *Component Placement* — arranging everything on the board for
   minimal trace lengths, clean power delivery, and good thermal
   performance.
3. *Routing* — connecting all the nets. This was the hardest part.
   Two-layer board, mixed-signal (audio and digital), careful ground
   plane management, keeping audio traces away from switching noise.
4. *Design Rule Checking (DRC)* — making sure the board meets
   manufacturing constraints: trace width, clearance, via size,
   silkscreen readability.
5. *Gerber Generation* — exporting the manufacturing files and
   sending them off to the fab.

The board is a two-layer design with a solid ground plane on the
bottom layer, star-ground topology for the audio section, and
dedicated power pours for the high-current paths.

* The Enclosure

The teleVox enclosure is a 3D printed shell designed in Fusion 360:

- Two-part clamshell design with screw bosses
- Cutouts for the speaker grille, microphone port, buttons, and USB-C
- Snap-fit battery compartment for easy cell swapping
- Designed for FDM printing with minimal supports
